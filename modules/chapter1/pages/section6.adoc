= Summary & Next Steps
:navtitle: Summary
:toc: macro
:description: Outcomes, caveats, and next steps for llm-d on OpenShift AI 3.x.

[.lead]
*You are no longer just "running a model." You are managing a distributed inference system.*

By deploying **Distributed Inference with llm-d** on Red Hat OpenShift AI 3.x, you have moved from static, single-pod serving to a platform that delivers:

* **Optimized TCO:** Intelligent scheduling and cache-aware routing let you pack more models and users onto the same hardware and lower cost-per-token.
* **Predictable performance:** Separation of prefill and decode (in advanced topologies) and load-aware routing keep latency consistent under load.
* **Sovereign control:** Data and prompts stay inside your OpenShift perimeter.

== Implementation notes (OpenShift AI 3.x)

* **Status in the UI:** Models may briefly show *Failed* in the dashboard while initializing. Check pod logs; status often flips to *Started* when ready.
* **Topologies:** Multi-node on certain hardware (e.g. GB200) may not be supported. Wide Expert-Parallelism (multi-node) may be in Developer Preview; confirm in product documentation.

== Next steps

* **Scale:** Increase `replicas` in your `LLMInferenceService` and re-apply; the Gateway and Scheduler will spread traffic.
* **MaaS:** llm-d is the recommended runtime for Models-as-a-Service (MaaS) in OpenShift AI 3.x; combine with Gateway API and Kuadrant for API-first, governed endpoints.
* **Governance:** Add TrustyAI and guardrails for safety and compliance once the inference path is stable.
