Day 2: Observability & Proving ROI

You cannot optimize what you cannot measure.

Day 1 is deploying the model. Day 2 is where the bill and the metrics show up. In a shadow-AI setup, teams fly blind until users complain. In the AI factory, observability is built in: you can see exactly what your llm-d workloads consume and prove GPU ROI.

Golden Signals of Distributed Inference

Beyond basic CPU/RAM, focus on these vLLM/llm-d signals:

  • KV cache usage: If KV cache is consistently near full (e.g. >95%), workers may pause to swap; consider more replicas or a lower --max-model-len.

  • TPOT (vllm_llmd_time_per_output_token_seconds): Generation speed.

  • TTFT (vllm_llmd_time_to_first_token_seconds): Time until the model starts responding; the scheduler optimizes for this by routing to the least-burdened pods.

  • GPU utilization (vllm_llmd_gpu_utilization_seconds): Confirms GPUs are doing work, not idling.

Workload metrics in the dashboard

OpenShift AI 3.x provides workload metrics for your projects. In the dashboard, go to Observe & monitor → Workload metrics, select your project (e.g. llmd-deploy-lab), and review Top resource-consuming distributed workloads. You can confirm CPU, memory, and GPU usage and that load is distributed across replicas.

Kueue and alerts

llm-d handles routing; Kueue handles who gets GPU quota. When you hit hardware or quota limits, Kueue queues workloads and can fire alerts. In Observe → Alerting, watch for:

  • KueuePodDown: Kueue pod not ready for a sustained period; no new workloads can be admitted.

  • PendingWorkloadPods: Pods pending for a long time; cluster may be out of GPUs or quota.

  • ResourceReservationExceedsQuota: Requests far exceed allowed quota; review manifests and quotas.

  • LowClusterQueueResourceUsage: Queue usage low for a long time; consider reallocating quota.

Use the Runbook links in the alert details for recommended actions.
